Bottom Line Up Front

Tax advisory has three failure modes, and ITA 1.0 has all of them. The first is computational: tax strategies never operate on a single line of the return. Every strategy simultaneously moves AGI, QBI, passive income, NIIT exposure, Social Security taxability, and IRMAA thresholds — all of which interact with each other at different rates and different income levels. The net saving of any strategy is only visible when the full return is held in memory and recomputed. The second failure mode is upstream of the engine entirely: 75% of ITA 1.0 customer feedback involves inputs that are missing, stale, bundled, or locked — and there is no static UI solution to an input space that spans hundreds of fields across clients with categorically different financial situations. The third forecloses the future entirely: an engine written in a domain-specific language no longer supported by its vendor, understood by exactly two engineers at Intuit, that incorrectly triggers AMT — a failure already responsible for a documented $20,000 client harm.

The answer to all three failure modes turns out to be the same architecture applied at different layers. The LLM handles computation and natural language input — dollar-exact against ProConnect, with a smaller intentional data model (that potentially supports MCP access into Intuit's existing tax engines where required). RAG provides the rates, limits, and thresholds the LLM cannot be trusted to recall across tax years. Strategy skill files bound hallucination by defining what a valid output must contain. Lacerte via MCP handles multi-year projections, state returns, and entity modeling — without rebuilding what Lacerte already maintains. Each component does what it is best at. The known weakness of each is covered by another layer.

ITA 1.0 is like a financial advisor who gives precise recommendations based on information from your last meeting, cannot accept new information unless it matches a form they already have, and whose training manual is written in a language only two of their colleagues can still read. Every year the tax code changes, the manual falls further behind — and every client whose situation falls outside the form library gets advice based on data that was never theirs.

01 · The Problem

Three failures that compound

Each failure is significant on its own. Together they describe a system that cannot give accurate advice, cannot accept the inputs that would make advice accurate, and cannot be maintained or extended to support what the market now requires.

Failure 1 The inputs are wrong before the calculation starts 75% of VOC involves inputs — missing, stale, bundled, or non-overridable
▼

Seventy-five percent of ITA 1.0 VOC involves inputs — missing, stale, bundled, or locked. This is not a complaint about the UI. It is a structural impossibility: the input space required to serve a median PTG client spans hundreds of fields across sole props, S-corp shareholders, real estate investors, retirees with complex distributions, and pass-through partners — each with their own relevant subset and their own natural language for describing their tax situation. There is no static form that covers all of them, and every prior attempt to solve this by adding more fields simply moved the complaints to the sections that hadn't yet been updated. The four failure modes are not independent edge cases. They are consequences of trying to approximate an unbounded input space with a fixed data model.

01
Missing inputs
Prior year values that cannot be updated flow directly into the engine unchanged. Detailed retirement distributions, investment details, and inputs outside the data model simply aren't there. The engine computes with confidence on data that was never the client's.
02
Bundled inputs
Multiple line items aggregated into a single adjustable number. "I didn't have that large advertising expense this year" becomes a multi-step decomposition exercise — not a description of the client's situation.
03
Non-overridable inputs
Fields visible but unchangeable. The gap between what the client described and what the engine received widens silently, with no error and no warning.
04
The full-field impossibility
ITA 1.0's answer — full input views per section — shifted complaints to whichever sections hadn't yet been updated. This problem has no static answer. The client base is too diverse; the domain too wide.
Failure 2 Tax strategies interact — the net saving is only visible when the full return recomputes $20K in excess advance tax from a single incorrect AMT trigger
▼

Every tax strategy operates on the same shared substrate: AGI, MAGI, taxable income, QBI, passive income, capital gains, Social Security taxability, NIIT, and AMT. None of these inputs moves in isolation. Reduce SE income with an S-corp election and you reduce QBI — losing part of the 20% deduction. A strategy that appears to save $8,000 in SE tax may cost $3,000 in lost QBI deduction, making S-corp conversion net-neutral or net-negative below roughly $80,000 of net profit. Accelerate a depreciation deduction and you reduce QBI again, create future recapture at ordinary rates, and potentially suspend losses in a passive entity where they cannot be used for years.

The interactions cut both ways. A $30,000 retirement contribution for a taxpayer sitting just above an IRMAA Medicare surcharge tier or a QBI phaseout threshold can save nearly twice its face-value tax — simultaneously reducing ordinary income and unlocking a deduction or eliminating a surcharge tier that the isolated calculation never sees. A rules-based system that evaluates strategies independently will overstate benefits when interactions reduce them and miss value entirely when interactions amplify them. In both cases the client is harmed — once by a bad recommendation, once by an opportunity never surfaced. The cost of not having the engine is systematically understated because missed upside is invisible.

Failure 3 The engine cannot be maintained — and forecloses everything the market now requires 2 engineers understand the DSL — vendor support ended
▼

The ITA planner — the component responsible for projections — is written in a domain-specific language no longer supported by its vendor and understood by exactly two engineers at Intuit. Every federal and state conformity update must pass through this bottleneck. The cost is high enough that the team consistently accepts known defects rather than implement them. This is not a resource problem. It is a structural impossibility: the wrongness compounds over time as each conformity cycle the system misses becomes a permanent divergence from current law.

Real-world consequence — documented AMT triggering — whether alternative minimum tax applies, and on what — is determined by the actual return in ProConnect, not by ITA's internal model. ITA cannot replicate the triggering logic without rebuilding it inside the DSL that nobody can maintain. The result: AMT incorrectly triggers in some cases and fails to trigger in others. A tax professional using ITA's projection recommended that a client pay $20,000 in excess advance tax — a direct, documented harm to a real client, caused by an architectural constraint that has no fix within the current system.

And this is before reaching the capabilities the market now requires. Multi-year projections — essential for S-corp conversion breakevens, Roth conversion ladders, retirement distribution sequencing, and QOZ hold-period decisions — require a planner that can carry inputs, tax law, and entity structure forward across multiple years simultaneously. S-corp and C-corp projections require modeling payroll, distributions, reasonable compensation, and accumulated earnings across periods. State returns require annual conformity implementation for every state in which clients operate. There is no conceivable path to any of this on a DSL that two people understand and no vendor supports. The choice is not between the current engine and a new one. It is between a new engine and the gradual exit from every use case that requires accurate, maintainable, multi-year, multi-entity tax computation. That is the entire addressable market for serious tax advisory.

02 · The Solution

The same architecture, applied differently at each layer

The answer to all three failure modes turns out to be the same architecture. The LLM handles computation and natural language input. RAG handles the rates and rules the LLM cannot recall. Skills handle strategy guardrails. Lacerte via MCP handles multi-year and state projections. Each component does what it is best at. The known weakness of each is covered by a different layer.

Layer 01
LLM Tax Engine
Dollar-exact accuracy against ProConnect returns — validated on 1040 Federal including QBI phaseouts, AMT, multi-year MACR depreciation carry-forward, 1120S passthrough income, and full cascading effects. More significantly, the LLM achieves this without requiring every input to be given explicitly: it infers the appropriate adjustments from whatever context it receives, applying the same logic a skilled preparer would apply when a client says "I had a large advertising expense last year that I won't have this year." The entire return adjusts. The computational accuracy problem and the natural language input problem are solved by the same capability.
Solves · strategy interaction modeling  ·  natural language input  ·  full-return recomputation
Layer 02
RAG — Rates, Limits & Thresholds
The LLM's most consistent failure mode is conflating rates, phase-out thresholds, and contribution limits across tax years. The mitigation is straightforward: an internal RAG containing current-year and projected-year rates, limits, thresholds, and select rules, retrieved at inference time. The LLM is not asked to remember that the QBI phaseout begins at $191,950 for single filers in 2024 — it is given that number when it needs it. This converts a memorization problem into a retrieval problem, which is both more reliable and auditable.
Solves · year-specific accuracy  ·  rate and threshold conformity
Layer 03
Skills — Strategy Guardrails
Every tax strategy can be expressed as a set of return items that must move, in what direction, by what mechanism. An S-corp conversion skill specifies that SE income decreases, W-2 wages appear, QBI recalculates, and the QBI deduction adjusts accordingly — giving the engine a verification framework for its own output. Where the LLM computes a result that violates the expected return signature, it is flagged before reaching the advisor. The guardrail doesn't prevent the LLM from reasoning about the strategy — it prevents it from returning an answer that is internally inconsistent with the strategy's known mechanics.
Solves · hallucination prevention  ·  strategy output verification
Layer 04
MCP → Lacerte Planner
The Lacerte planner already solves multi-year projections — modeling multiple years with proper indexing of rate changes, standard deduction adjustments, and bracket inflation. State conformity lives inside Lacerte, maintained by the teams that have always maintained it. MCP integration surfaces Lacerte's output as a tool the LLM engine can call, rather than attempting to rebuild that computation inside a new system. Multi-year projections, S-corp modeling, C-corp projections, and state returns are solved by the system that already solves them correctly. ITA 2.0 does not need to solve state conformity. It needs to stop duplicating it.

03 · Why Now

The accuracy threshold has been crossed

The LLM tax engine is not a bet on future capability. The core validation — dollar-exact agreement with ProConnect — is complete for 1040 Federal.

This has been demonstrated across the full range of 1040 complexity: QBI with income-based phaseouts, AMT with ISO stock option triggers, multi-year MACR depreciation where ITA 1.0 copies the first-year value rather than computing the correct year-in-service percentage, 1120S passthrough income with basis limitations, and full cascading effects where a depreciation deduction drives tax liability to zero and converts non-refundable credits to refundable. These are not clean test cases. They are the exact scenarios where ITA 1.0 either returns the wrong answer or cannot be updated to return the right one.

Two properties of the LLM make this moment different from every prior attempt to modernize the engine. First, the LLM operates on input descriptions rather than input fields — it does not require the data model to grow before it can accept a new fact about the client's situation. Second, its known failure mode is narrow and bounded: it conflates rates and thresholds across years. That problem is solved by retrieval. Every prior attempt at engine replacement failed because it required rebuilding the entire input model and the entire computation simultaneously. This architecture separates those two problems and solves them independently. Both are now solved.

Taken together: the LLM handles the computation and the input layer. RAG handles the rates and rules the LLM cannot be trusted to recall. Skills handle strategy guardrails. Lacerte via MCP handles multi-year and state projections. The result is an architecture where each component does what it is best at, the known weaknesses of each are covered by a different layer, and the whole produces something ITA 1.0 cannot: an accurate, maintainable, extensible tax advisory engine that accepts how clients actually describe their situation and returns the true net impact of every decision across their full return.

Intuit's differentiated advantage The advantage is not the model. It is the validation loop. Running the LLM in shadow mode against deterministic ProConnect output — using prior-year returns as ground truth, with tax professionals as the continuous human signal — creates a feedback flywheel no competitor can replicate. The data and the client relationships already exist. The architecture to exploit them is what Project AIR is building.
